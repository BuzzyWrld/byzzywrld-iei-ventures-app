# Color Theory Reference — Brand Playbook

This reference is loaded by the brand-playbook skill during **Step 2: Design the Brand Kit**,
specifically when selecting and justifying the brand color palette. The LLM must cross-reference
three axes before assigning any color: (1) color psychology, (2) industry archetype, (3) target
audience demographics. Never select colors by aesthetic preference alone.

---

## How to Use This Reference

When building the color palette for any brand:

1. Identify the **industry archetype** (see Section 2)
2. Profile the **target audience** (see Section 3)
3. Map both against the **color psychology table** (see Section 1)
4. Resolve conflicts using the **hierarchy rule** (audience > industry > aesthetic)
5. Assign each color in the palette with a written justification citing all three axes
6. Apply the **contrast and accessibility rules** (see Section 4)

Every color in the final palette must have a one-line rationale in this format:
> `[Color Name] [Hex] — [Psychology trigger] × [Industry signal] × [Audience resonance]`

Example:
> `Forest Authority #182F23 — trust + permanence (psychology) × institutional finance (industry) × 35–55yo professional men who associate dark green with legacy wealth (audience)`

## Section 1: Color Psychology Table

### Primary Psychological Triggers by Color Family

| Color Family | Core Triggers | Shadow Side (overuse risk) |
|---|---|---|
| **Deep Navy / Midnight Blue** | Trust, authority, stability, intelligence, depth | Cold, impersonal, bureaucratic |
| **Dark Green / Forest** | Legacy, wealth, permanence, growth, old money | Stagnant, institutional, unapproachable |
| **Teal / Cyan** | Innovation, clarity, calm, modernity, approachability | Sterile, tech-bro generic |
| **Warm Gold / Amber** | Prestige, achievement, warmth, optimism, aspiration | Gaudy, cheap if overused |
| **Muted Gold / Antique** | Heritage, wisdom, quality, timelessness, gravitas | Dated if used with wrong typeface |
| **Burgundy / Deep Red** | Power, passion, urgency, luxury, appetite | Aggression, danger if too saturated |
| **Burnt Orange / Terracotta** | Energy, creativity, warmth, cultural richness | Loud, informal, dates quickly |
| **Deep Indigo / Violet** | Creativity, mystery, spiritual depth, premium | Niche, polarizing, can alienate B2B |
| **Charcoal / Near-Black** | Sophistication, control, modern luxury, editorial | Heavy, oppressive without relief |
| **Ivory / Parchment / Cream** | Heritage, editorial, calm, literary quality, warmth | Passive, low energy without contrast |
| **Pure White** | Minimalism, clarity, clinical precision, openness | Generic, cold, zero personality |
| **Sage / Muted Green** | Wellness, balance, sustainability, calm, nature | Weak, trend-chasing if not earned |
| **Blush / Rose** | Femininity, softness, approachability, care | Limiting demographic reach, infantilizing |
| **Electric Blue / Cobalt** | Innovation, confidence, digital-native, disruption | Overused in tech, startup fatigue |
| **Coral / Warm Pink** | Youthful energy, optimism, casual, consumer-friendly | Playful to the point of unprofessional |
| **Silver / Cool Gray** | Technology, precision, neutrality, modern craft | Lifeless, uncommitted |
| **Black (true)** | Absolute authority, luxury, fashion editorial, premium | Inaccessible, heavy for light-touch brands |

### Saturation Rules

- **High saturation**: Triggers urgency, emotion, youth appeal — use for consumer, lifestyle, food/beverage brands
- **Mid saturation**: Balanced trust + energy — professional services, healthcare, education
- **Low/muted saturation**: Heritage, sophistication, editorial — luxury, finance, literary, legal

### Brightness Rules

- **Light / high brightness**: Approachability, digital-native, modern — B2C, wellness, consumer apps
- **Dark / low brightness**: Authority, gravitas, permanence — B2B, institutional, luxury
- **High contrast palette**: Urgency, modernity, strong brand confidence
- **Low contrast palette**: Editorial calm, softness, trust — but risks illegibility

## Section 2: Industry Color Archetypes

Cross-reference the client's industry with the anchoring color expectations for that space.
These are NOT rules to follow blindly — they are the category baseline the audience brings
into every interaction. Deviating from them is a strategic choice that must be intentional.

### Finance & Wealth Management
- **Anchors**: Deep green, navy, charcoal, gold
- **Why**: Centuries of association with stability, growth, and conservative wealth stewardship
- **Opportunity deviation**: A boutique advisory firm targeting young entrepreneurs can shift to teal + cream to signal modernity within the trust category
- **Danger zone**: Bright red, neon, high-saturation colors — signals volatility or immaturity

### Legal & Professional Services
- **Anchors**: Navy, charcoal, gold, white
- **Why**: Authority, precision, trust — the stakes are high and the brand must signal it
- **Opportunity deviation**: Boutique firms targeting creatives/startups can use dark indigo to stay within the authority zone while feeling less corporate
- **Danger zone**: Anything playful, pastel, or warm-casual

### Healthcare & Wellness
- **Anchors**: Soft teal, sage, clean white, light blue, muted green
- **Why**: Calm, cleanliness, care, clinical reliability
- **Opportunity deviation**: Luxury wellness / concierge health can shift to gold, ivory, charcoal for premium positioning
- **Danger zone**: Red (emergency, pain), pure black (death/grief), high-saturation colors (anxiety)

### Technology & SaaS
- **Anchors**: Near-black, electric blue, cobalt, white, silver
- **Why**: Precision, intelligence, digital-native confidence
- **Opportunity deviation**: AI/ML companies targeting enterprise can go dark + gold to signal premium over generic startup blue
- **Danger zone**: Warm colors (unprofessional in B2B tech), pastel (childlike), brown (pre-digital)

### Real Estate & Construction
- **Anchors**: Dark green, navy, charcoal, gold, earthy warm tones
- **Why**: Stability, permanence, trust, physical quality
- **Sub-segment note**: Luxury residential → charcoal + gold + ivory. Commercial/construction → dark green + navy + silver. Affordable housing → warm teal + white + orange

### Food & Beverage
- **Anchors**: Red, orange, yellow (appetite triggers), brown/earth (craft/artisan), green (fresh/healthy)
- **Why**: Red and orange are proven appetite stimulants (McDonald's, Coca-Cola, Chipotle)
- **Opportunity deviation**: Premium/luxury food → black + gold + burgundy. Health food → muted green + cream + terracotta
- **Danger zone**: Blue (appetite suppressant — avoid for food unless intentional, like water/seafood)

### Beauty & Personal Care
- **Anchors**: Varies widely by positioning — black/gold (luxury), pink/rose (feminine mass market), white/clean (clinical/minimalist), sage (clean beauty)
- **Key rule**: The palette signals the price point as much as anything else
- **Danger zone**: Overly masculine color schemes for female-targeted brands (and vice versa) unless disruption is the intent

### Education & Publishing
- **Anchors**: Navy, gold, ivory, deep burgundy, forest green
- **Why**: Academic heritage, intellectual authority, permanence
- **Opportunity deviation**: EdTech targets younger audiences — shift to electric blue + white + orange for energy while retaining authority

### Consumer Lifestyle / Streetwear / Culture
- **Anchors**: No anchors — category is defined by deliberate deviation from establishment colors
- **Rule**: Color choice is a cultural statement. Study competitor palettes and position relationally

### Nonprofit / Social Impact
- **Anchors**: Warm teal, sage, gold, white, warm orange
- **Why**: Trustworthiness + approachability + optimism — signals cause-driven, people-first
- **Danger zone**: Dark/corporate palettes (signals cold, transactional), pure red (alarm/emergency unless cause-specific)

## Section 3: Target Audience Color Resonance

Different demographic profiles respond differently to the same colors. After assigning industry
anchors, layer in the audience profile. If there's a conflict, **audience wins.**

### By Age Group

| Audience | Color Preferences | Avoid |
|---|---|---|
| **Gen Z (18–26)** | High contrast, muted/desaturated "aesthetic" palettes, unexpected color combinations, dark mode-native | Boomer palettes (navy/khaki), overly polished corporate looks |
| **Millennials (27–42)** | Clean modern palettes, teal/sage, warm neutrals, millennial pink, mid-saturation | Dated corporate (dark navy + red), overly masculine or feminine palettes |
| **Gen X (43–58)** | Trust signals — navy, green, gold, classic editorial palettes | Trendy gimmick colors, anything that looks like it was designed for a younger audience |
| **Boomers (59+)** | Traditional palettes, high contrast for readability, warm tones, classic heritage | Dark low-contrast, neon, abstract color choices without clear hierarchy |
| **C-Suite / Institutional** | Conservative anchors but with a quality signal — dark + gold, navy + white, charcoal + accent | Anything that reads as startup, playful, or low-budget |

### By Gender (general tendencies — always validate against specific ICA)

- **Primarily female audiences**: Warm tones, rose, blush, sage, cream — but luxury female audiences skew black + gold + ivory (Chanel principle)
- **Primarily male audiences**: Cool blues, dark greens, charcoal, navy — but lifestyle/consumer male audiences accept warm amber, terracotta
- **Gender-neutral**: Teal, muted gold, near-black, electric blue, clean white

### By Income / Wealth Band

| Wealth Tier | Palette Signal |
|---|---|
| **Mass market / Value** | High saturation, bold contrast, warm urgency colors (red, orange, yellow) |
| **Aspirational / Middle** | Mid-saturation, modern palettes, teal/navy/warm neutrals — premium feel without intimidation |
| **Affluent / Upper-middle** | Muted, sophisticated, low contrast — signals "we don't need to shout" |
| **Ultra-high-net-worth / Institutional** | Monochromatic dark fields with single metallic accent — implies absolute confidence |

### By Geographic / Cultural Context

- **US & Northern Europe**: Trust associations with blue strongest; green = nature/wellness or finance depending on shade
- **Middle East / South Asia**: Gold is prestige signal; green carries religious significance — use deliberately
- **East Asia**: Red = luck/celebration (NOT danger); white = mourning in some contexts (NOT purity)
- **Latin America**: High saturation reads as vibrant/festive — muted palettes may read as cold

## Section 4: Contrast, Accessibility, and Pairing Rules

### WCAG Contrast Standards (always check)
- **Text on background minimum**: 4.5:1 ratio (normal text), 3:1 (large text/headlines)
- **Recommended**: Aim for 7:1 on body copy for professional readability
- **Quick check rule**: If you can't read it comfortably at 50% zoom, the contrast fails

### Contrast Pairs That Always Work
- Dark forest green (#182F23) + Parchment (#F5F0E6) ✓
- Deep navy (#0A1628) + Warm white (#F8F6F0) ✓
- Charcoal (#1C1C1E) + Gold (#D4AF37) ✓ (use gold for headlines only — not body)
- Deep indigo (#0C0030) + Old gold (#C4A35A) ✓
- Near-black (#08070E) + Electric accent (#4A9EFF) ✓ (tech/digital)

### Contrast Pairs That Fail
- Gold on white — insufficient contrast, reads as faded
- Mid-gray on light gray — passes accessibility threshold but fails visual clarity
- Teal on green — adjacent hues bleed into each other
- Navy on black — too close in value to read clearly

### Color Count Rules
- **Minimum palette**: 6 colors (Primary, Secondary, Accent, Background, Text, Muted/Neutral)
- **Maximum for brand coherence**: 8 colors (add Surface and Divider)
- **Active colors in any single layout**: No more than 3 at once — one dominant (60%), one secondary (30%), one accent (10%)
- **60-30-10 rule**: Background = 60%, secondary/supporting element = 30%, accent = 10%

### Color Weight Hierarchy in Layouts
1. Background / field color establishes the base tone
2. Primary text color must contrast the background at 7:1 minimum
3. Accent color is the only color that can sit on either dark or light fields — it must work on both
4. Muted/neutral is used for secondary text, captions, dividers — never for primary communication

## Section 5: Color Justification Output Format

When presenting the palette in the playbook, every color gets a justification card. Format:

```
COLOR NAME: [Name]
HEX: [#000000]
RGB: [r, g, b]
ROLE: [Primary / Secondary / Accent / Background / Text / Muted]
PSYCHOLOGY: [Core trigger — what this color does to the viewer's brain]
INDUSTRY SIGNAL: [What this color says in the [industry] context]
AUDIENCE FIT: [Why this color resonates with [target demographic]]
USAGE RULE: [Where and how to use it — what it's forbidden on]
```

This block appears in the Style Guide section of the playbook alongside the rendered swatch.
Never omit the justification. If the color was client-specified and doesn't align with theory,
flag it: `[Client-specified — note: this deviates from [X theory] — confirm intent]`
