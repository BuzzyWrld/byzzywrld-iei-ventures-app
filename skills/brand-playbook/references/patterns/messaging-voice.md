# Messaging & Voice Patterns

> Distilled from 8 IEI training documents. Use this when generating brand voice, communication persona, ad copy, sales scripts, creator briefs, or any worksheet that asks "how should this brand sound."

## Core principles

The IEI house voice is built on one premise: **the brand is a high-status diagnostician, not a vendor**. The reader is a peer. We diagnose, we prescribe, we provide receipts. We do not pitch, hype, or apologize for our authority. Every sentence should pass three filters before it ships:

1. **The "Informed Sibling" filter** — Does this sound like the friend who actually read the 50-page report so the rest of the group didn't have to? Calm, direct, blunt, data-backed, slightly amused at the industry's current nonsense.
2. **The "Information Gain" filter** — Does this sentence leave the reader smarter than they were before, even if they never buy? If you stripped the brand name, would there still be a unique perspective or piece of evidence in the line?
3. **The "Cringe Test"** — Would the Ideal Client Avatar (e.g., "Jordan") share this, or scroll past because it feels like an ad? If it feels promotional, it's broken.

The brand is always **fighting an Enemy**. The Enemy is a specific industry behavior — corporate media spin, mass-produced fast fashion, generic agency-speak, AI slop, parasitic discount sites. House voice constantly references that Enemy, gently or aggressively, to draw the line between "us" and "them." Without an Enemy, copy sounds like neutral branding fluff.

The brand is always **protective of its own time and standards**. We don't beg for the sale. We don't "love to hear from you." We say "we only take 2 clients per quarter to protect quality" and we mean it. The voice is comfortable walking away. That comfort is the source of its authority.

## Frameworks

### The "Informed Sibling" Persona

The single most important persona across IEI work. Used for sales nurture, ad copy, blog content, and creator briefs.

**Definition:** "You are the one who actually read the 50-page report so your friends didn't have to." Fast-paced, direct, no-nonsense, high eye contact. The smartest person in the room who refuses to perform that fact.

**Three Pillars:**
- **Ruthless Candor** — If a prospect's ads are trash, we don't say "sub-optimal." We say exactly why they're burning money and who is lying to them.
- **Information Gain** — Every interaction leaves the reader smarter, even if they never buy. We share the "Internal Receipts" that other agencies hide.
- **Protective of Time** — No "pick your brain" calls. Time is the most valuable non-renewable resource.

**Power Dynamic:** Always the Partner Frame, never the Vendor Frame.

| Element | Vendor Frame (weak) | Partner Frame (Informed Sibling) |
|---|---|---|
| Goal | Get the "Yes" at any cost | Find the truth and verify fit |
| Tone | Overly polite, service-oriented | Blunt, direct, data-backed |
| Reaction to "No" | Defensiveness or discounting | Indifference. "We aren't for everyone." |
| Status | Subordinate (waiting for approval) | Peer/authority (leading the process) |

**Vocabulary swaps to enforce:**
- Avoid: "I was just checking in" → Use: "Here is the leak in your funnel"
- Avoid: "Does this price work for you?" → Use: "Does it make sense to fix this now, or are you okay with the current burn rate?"
- Avoid: "We'd love to work with you" → Use: "We only take on 2 clients per quarter to protect quality"

### The "OG" Persona (variant)

Used when the brand is closer to lifestyle/streetwear/heritage than media/info. "You are the friend who was into this before it was cool. You value the stitch over the logo." Calm, intentional, low-voice, focus on details. No "hype-beast" energy. Insider knowledge over loud advocacy.

### The Brand-to-Channel Translation Engine

Voice is not platform-agnostic. The brand persona stays constant, but the **execution rhythm** must translate. Claude must cross-reference the brand persona against each platform's "Algorithmic Currency."

| Platform | Algorithmic Currency | Voice Translation |
|---|---|---|
| TikTok | Watch time (first 3s) + Shares + Audio trends | Raw and native. Strip corporate polish. Strong, immediate text-on-screen hooks. Visceral aesthetic vibes. |
| Instagram (Reels/Grid) | Saves + Shares + DM forwards | Curated and nurturing. High visual fidelity. Carousels educational or aesthetic. Captions formatting-heavy. |
| IG Stories / Snapchat | Completion rate + Direct replies | Urgent and unfiltered. Countdowns, behind-the-scenes, polls. Native stickers. |
| YouTube (long-form) | CTR + Average View Duration | Deep authority. Chapters, deep dives. Persona = subject matter expert. |
| X / Threads | Replies + Quote reposts | Punchy and polarizing. Short, sharp takes. Memetic. Brand tone in rapid-fire text without sounding like a bot. |
| LinkedIn | Dwell time + Meaningful comments | Founder-led. "How we built this" narrative. Systematized struggles and wins. |
| Discord / Reddit | Genuine contribution + Non-promotional engagement | Community first. Conversation starters, not marketing copy. Peer-to-peer. |

**Rule:** Don't write TikTok scripts that sound like LinkedIn posts. Don't write LinkedIn posts that sound like ad copy. The brand persona is the constant; the rhythm is the variable.

### The Vibe Dictionary (Green-Light vs Red-Light Words)

Every brand gets a per-brand list of approved and forbidden words. Creators, copywriters, and the brand's own social voice all operate inside it.

**Green-Light Words (signs of belonging) — examples from IEI's own brands:**
- OffScript (streetwear): Archive, Industrial, Modular, Heavyweight, Reference, Script-flip, 1-of-1, Deadstock, GSM
- AceTV+ (media literacy): Receipts, Signal-to-noise, Deep-dive, Deconstructed, PR-spin, Media-literacy, The truth

**Red-Light Words (signs of a sell-out / kill the vibe instantly):**
- "Collaboration," "Brand Deal," "Sponsored"
- "Amazing," "Game-changer," "Obsessed"
- "Cool," "Buy now," "Discount," "Influencer," "Collab"

When generating a brand playbook, always produce both lists. The Green-Light list is the "secret handshake" that signals the writer belongs in the brand's universe. The Red-Light list prevents the dilution that kills audiences faster than bad strategy.

### The Ad Copy "Anti-Brief" Logic

Ad copy and creator scripts at IEI follow the **Modular Brief** structure, not a word-for-word script. The creator is the director; the brand is Executive Producer.

- **The 70/30 Rule** — 70% of the asset is the creator's native style (lighting, pacing, slang). 30% is the brand's non-negotiables (specific Hook, Product Shot, CTA).
- **The Hook (0–3s)** — Brand-required. Provide 3 specific text-on-screen hooks; creator films 3 versions.
- **The Body (15–45s)** — Creator-flexible. Brand provides 3 talking points; creator uses their own words.
- **The CTA (final 5s)** — Brand-required. Specific bridge phrase (e.g., "Hit the link in bio to join the next 1-of-1 drop").
- **The "No-Ring-Light" Mandate** — For raw brands, content must look "captured," not "produced." Handheld, natural light, raw audio. iPhone mic, not a studio mic, for "authentic peer" voice.
- **The Jump-Cut Cadence** — Visual change every 2.5–3 seconds.

### The Truth-Seeker Discovery Script (Sales)

For diagnostic sales conversations. The salesperson doesn't pitch — they make the prospect "reveal their own wounds" so the seller can heal them.

- Ask "Cost of Inaction" questions.
- **The Silent Pause Rule:** After asking a Cost-of-Inaction question, remain silent. Let the pain settle. Do not rescue them from the discomfort.
- Reframe price/time objections back to the Enemy (the status quo).
- Walk away first. The "Takeaway" / Disqualification builds more trust than a "yes" ever could: *"Based on our audit, your team isn't ready for the scale of our Paid Ads Engine. We could take your money, but you wouldn't see the ROI we demand for our partners. Come back to us when you've fixed [X] and [Y]."*

### The "Anti-Check-In" Nurture Mandate

Banned phrases in any follow-up: "Just checking in," "Touching base," "Any update?" Every follow-up must contain a **Value Ping** that gives the prospect a reason to thank you for the email.

Three types of Value Pings:
- **The "Enemy" Move** — *"I just saw your top competitor launched [X]. Their hook is weak, but they're stealing your 'Commercial Intent' traffic."* (Triggers fear of being left behind.)
- **The "New Tech" Insight** — *"We just ran a test on a new AI video script for another partner. It lowered their CAC by 12%. I think we should apply this to your 1-of-1 Drop."* (Proves we're constantly innovating while they "think.")
- **The "Internal Receipt"** — *"I just published this deep dive on how we fixed the retention leak for [Similar Brand]. Thought you'd find page 4 relevant."* (Social proof without bragging.)

After 14 days of silence, send the **Sunset Email** to reclaim status:
> "It's been two weeks since our audit, and I haven't heard back. I'm assuming priorities have shifted or you've decided to stick with the current team. I'm going to close your file and release the slot in our Creative Engine to another brand on the waitlist. If things change down the road, feel free to reach out. Best of luck with the scale."

This often triggers a "Panic Reply." The Informed Sibling does not chase forever; they close the file themselves.

## House voice / patterns

**Sentence rhythm.** Short, declarative, occasionally truncated for impact. Mix one long sentence (a full thought, comma-managed) with two or three short ones. Cadence over decoration. Avoid the long compound-complex sentences that make corporate copy unreadable. Don't be afraid of one-word lines — *"Ignore."* *"Kill it."* *"Receipts."*

**Address the reader directly.** Use "you" and "we." Never "the consumer," "the user base," "our valued partners."

**Use receipts and specifics, not adjectives.** "400 GSM" not "heavyweight." "22% lower CAC" not "much better performance." "Two clients per quarter" not "highly selective." Specificity is the proof.

**First-person mandate** (for content): Every article should contain at least two instances of "We found that..." or "In our experience..." The "internal receipt" — naming a fabric mill, citing GSM test results, referencing a debunked media bias — is what separates IEI content from generic SEO slop.

**Counter-Narrative Check.** Every piece of substantive content should challenge at least one mainstream industry assumption. If the article doesn't push back on something, it's filler.

**The Pattern Interrupt.** Open emails, ads, and posts with something the reader doesn't expect — a competitor screenshot, a contrarian one-liner, a specific data point. Never open with "I hope this finds you well."

**The Comparison Move.** Most IEI sales/audit copy is structured "What they tell you" vs. "What we do." Two columns, no waffle:
- *"We're optimizing for ROAS"* → *"We optimize for EBITDA"*
- *"We're posting 3x a week"* → *"We build a Content Moat that kills your dependence on ads"*

**The Bluntness License.** Permission to say things like *"To be blunt, our Paid Ads Engine would likely break your current fulfillment, and you'd end up burning cash."* This is not rudeness. It is candor delivered without ego. The reader respects it.

**Reference-based feedback (when critiquing).** No "I don't like this." Always cite a specific rule: *"This headline violates Doc 4 because it lacks a Pattern Interrupt and doesn't use the Informed Sibling tone."* Even internal voice notes follow this discipline.

## Anti-patterns to avoid

**AI-isms — automatic rejection:**
- "In the ever-evolving landscape of..."
- "Delve into..."
- "Unlock your potential..."
- "In conclusion..."
- "It's important to note that..."
- "Navigate the complexities of..."
- "Game-changer," "revolutionary," "cutting-edge," "world-class"
- Three-item lists where every item is the same length and starts with the same part of speech (the AI rule-of-three tell)

**Corporate jargon — automatic rejection:**
- "Synergy," "holistic," "leverage" (as a verb, in a vague sense), "best-in-class," "value-add"
- "We're excited to announce..."
- "Stakeholders," "ecosystem of partners," "thought leader"
- "Solutions" used as a noun without specificity ("our solutions help you...")

**Sales-speak — automatic rejection:**
- "Just checking in" / "Touching base" / "Circling back"
- "I'd love to..." / "We're passionate about..."
- "Act now," "Don't miss out," excessive exclamation points
- "Free!" / "Guarantee!" / "$$$" — these also trigger spam filters

**Performative friendliness.** The brand is "kind when possible" but "never performatively nice." Empathy without muddying reality. We do not soften a clear diagnosis to spare feelings.

**Vanity claims without proof.** "Industry-leading," "trusted by thousands," "world-class results" — banned unless paired with a specific number, name, or screenshot.

**Vagueness about the Enemy.** If the copy doesn't have a clear "us vs them," it's not finished. Generic "we believe in quality" copy is invisible.

**Following trends literally.** When a competitor goes viral, we don't copy the trend verbatim. We extract the **mechanic** (e.g., "aggressive call-out of bad industry practice") and inject our DNA (e.g., "calmly dismantling the industry lie with receipts, empathy, and humor"). Mechanics over mimicry.

**Begging behavior.** "Please consider..." "Would you mind..." "If it's not too much trouble..." These are status-leaks. Removed from all outputs.

## Worked example

**The brief:** Write a 60-second ad script for a streetwear brand (OffScript-style) launching a heavyweight tee.

**Weak version (generic agency):**
> "Introducing the all-new OffScript Heavyweight Tee. Crafted with premium materials and unmatched attention to detail, this is more than a t-shirt — it's a statement. Available now in our latest drop. Don't miss out on this limited-edition release. Shop now and elevate your wardrobe."

Why it fails: "Introducing," "all-new," "premium," "unmatched," "more than a t-shirt," "elevate your wardrobe," "don't miss out." Every phrase is a Red-Light word. No Enemy. No receipts. No Pattern Interrupt. Anyone could have written this for any brand.

**IEI version:**
> "Most tees you buy are 180 GSM. They shrink. They sag. They look like an ad someone wrote on a plane. This one is 320, milled in Portugal, cut boxy on purpose. We made 200. When they're gone, they're gone — no restock, no email asking you to wait nicely. If that sounds like your speed, the link's in bio. If not, no hard feelings."

Why it works:
- Pattern Interrupt opening (specific GSM data, not adjectives).
- Names the Enemy (mass-produced fast fashion, "an ad someone wrote on a plane").
- Receipts (320 GSM, Portugal mill, 200 units).
- Green-Light vocabulary ("milled," "cut boxy," "restock").
- Walk-away energy ("no hard feelings"). The brand is not begging.
- Sentence rhythm: short / short / short / longer / one-word punchline ("milled in Portugal, cut boxy on purpose"). Cadence carries the line.
