**Purpose:** A separate, explicit consent that the customer ticks at checkout — distinct from the Terms of Service. ## Items Flagged for Attorney Attention

1. **Trade-secret protection language** — Section 2: I drafted as IP ownership plus contractual restrictions. Consider whether to add explicit trade-secret designation under the Defend Trade Secrets Act (federal) and relevant state Uniform Trade Secrets Act variants. This affects available remedies if there's a breach.
2. **Liquidated damages clause** — Section 7: consider adding a specified liquidated-damages amount for clear IP violations (e.g., copying the framework). Attorney call to discuss enforceability.
3. **Survival** — Section 6: I drafted as perpetual for ownership/confidentiality. Confirm enforceability period under state law.
4. **Two-party-state recording consent** — Section 4: even with consent capture, two-party-state laws may require additional confirmation language. Confirm process.
5. **Class-action waiver** — consider whether to add (and confirm enforceability in your jurisdiction post-recent Supreme Court decisions on B2C arbitration/waivers).
6. **Carve-outs for whistleblower / Defend Trade Secrets Act notice** — federal law requires specific notice in confidentiality agreements about whistleblower immunity (18 U.S.C. § 1833(b)). Recommend adding.
7. **What if a customer disputes ownership of an Output?** — e.g., they say "you used my idea for someone else." Confirm dispute-resolution path; consider mediation-first clause.
8. **Click-wrap enforceability** — modern courts generally enforce click-wrap with clear assent, but UX matters: the checkbox must be unchecked by default, the link to full text must be conspicuous, the user must take affirmative action. Confirm checkout UX meets these standards.
