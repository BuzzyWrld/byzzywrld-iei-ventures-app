Brand App — Go-To-Market & Bankroll Plan

An IEI Ventures innovation. A working plan to take the brand/marketing app from near-market MVP to revenue — with a licensing path for the music app. Prepared for Ashley Harris and Tabitha Wolod.

Portfolio context — IEI Ventures

IEI Ventures is the parent company. Its innovations arm holds a portfolio of products — the brand/marketing app, the music app, and VoltBay — each a product under the IEI Ventures parent.

Brand approach.  Each product goes to market under its own name and identity and leads in customer-facing materials. IEI Ventures stays behind the scenes as the parent company — named in legal, ownership, and investor contexts, not in day-to-day product marketing.

Why it helps.  A portfolio under one parent means shared infrastructure (legal, finance, brand standards) and a stronger story for investors and partners — IEI Ventures is a company with a pipeline, not a single bet.

To decide.  Confirm with an attorney whether each product should be its own LLC under IEI Ventures or a product line within it. This affects liability, licensing, and any future sale of a single product.

Where things stand

Brand app.  MVP ready, final testing underway, near market. Needs bankroll to launch.

Music app.  Simple final MVP built. Plan: license / white-label it — keep ownership, others pay to use it.

Strategy.  One-to-one sales and business cohorts first; enterprise, white-label, and licensing as it scales.

Brand app — the go-to-market sequence

With a near-market product, the discipline is simple: land paying customers fast, prove it works, then climb to larger deals. Move through these stages in order — do not skip ahead to enterprise before Stage 1 and 2 give you proof.

Stage 1 — One-to-one sales (start here)

Sell directly to individual customers. Fast to launch, small deal size, builds proof and early cash.

Needs: one clear price, a simple sign-up and checkout, and a repeatable short pitch.

Goal: a first handful of paying users and honest feedback before scaling spend.

Stage 2 — Business cohorts (the core motion)

Onboard groups of similar businesses together. Higher value per deal, efficient to sell and support at once.

Needs: a defined group offer, onboarding that scales, and one or two reference customers willing to vouch.

Goal: predictable repeatable revenue and proof points that unlock the bigger deals.

Stage 3 — Enterprise sales

Large organizations, larger contracts. Long cycles measured in months, with procurement and security review.

Needs: a security and data-handling summary, customer references, a contract template, and patience.

Price high enough that the long sales cycle is worth it.

Stage 4 — White-label & licensing

Other companies run the app under their own brand (white-label), or pay for the right to use the technology (licensing).

Strong recurring revenue, but requires clean brand separation and protective agreements (see IP & legal below).

Sequencing principle: Stages 1–2 prove demand and generate cash quickly. Use that proof and revenue to pursue Stages 3–4, which take longer but pay far more.



Bankroll — funding the launch

With a near-market MVP and early cohort demand, weigh these paths before assuming a raise is required. They are not mutually exclusive — several can run in parallel.

Revenue-funded (try this first)

Sell Stage 1 and Stage 2 customers, use that cash to fund the launch.

No equity given up, full ownership kept, and real revenue is the strongest proof for any future raise or partnership.

Customer-funded

An enterprise or white-label partner pays an upfront deal that bankrolls the launch.

The strongest position of all — a customer validates and funds you at the same time.

Non-dilutive capital

Grants, small-business competitions, accelerators, revenue-based financing.

Capital without giving up equity. Worth pursuing in parallel with sales.

Raise (angel / pre-seed)

Outside investment in exchange for equity. Faster capital, but you give up ownership.

Best only if growth is genuinely capital-constrained and the upside is large. A signed cohort or one enterprise deal first will dramatically improve the terms.

Recommendation: get to first revenue before raising. One signed cohort or enterprise deal changes both the bank balance and the negotiating power.

Music app — the licensing / white-label path

The plan is to keep ownership and let others pay to use it — not to sell it outright. This is usually the stronger wealth path for a built product: recurring revenue, the asset stays with IEI Ventures, and it can still be sold later, for more, from a position of strength.

License models to consider

White-label.  Another company runs the music app under their own brand for their users.

Usage / seat licensing.  Charge per user, per use, by term, or by territory for the right to use the technology.

Platform / API licensing.  A larger platform integrates your app's functionality and pays for it.

What licensing requires before any deal

A written licensing or white-label agreement defining scope, term, fees, and territory.

Clear IP ownership — IEI Ventures must own the app cleanly before licensing it out.

Clarity on who owns customer data and who handles support under a white-label arrangement.

Brand separation so a white-label partner's use does not dilute or confuse your own brand.

IP & legal — protect the assets first

Both apps become licensable assets only if the IP underneath them is owned and protected. Before any white-label or licensing deal:

Trademarks.  Register the product names and logos. Inexpensive, fast, and the core asset for a software product.

Copyright.  Software is automatically copyrighted; registration strengthens enforcement. The code is an asset.

Ownership.  Confirm IEI Ventures — not the individuals — owns the apps via written IP assignment agreements.

Agreements.  Use NDAs before sharing the product with any partner, and a proper licensing agreement before any white-label or licensing deal.

Patents.  Most software is not patentable. Pursue a patent only if a professional prior-art search confirms a genuine, non-obvious invention.

Days 31–60

Days 61–90

Open first music-app licensing conversations.

Decide the bankroll path based on actual revenue — revenue-funded, customer-funded, or raise.
